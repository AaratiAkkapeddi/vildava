Vildava ‘Death Content’ (2026)


cover photography by max branigan

On the heels of a year of touring and gestation at local shows in New York, Brooklyn based musician Dan Lawrence formally introduces his new hardware-electronic project Vildava, with the collection Death Content. Across six songs, the record crafts a sound-world of redlined drum machines, warbling arpeggiated synths and endless saturation, all carried by Lawrence’s urgent and unironically impassioned vocals. Death Content is a revelatory incitement of life in a time of dimmed prospects and dulled senses; launching itself explosively through the slop and content toward an unknown future, liberated and desperately alive.


“I always conceived of this record, and Vildava broadly, as a live project. The seeds of this album were sewn on club stages and basement floors, as minimal compositions for a pitch-tuned drum machine, mixer and voice; often with lyrics improvised on the spot. Over two years of honing live material, much now discarded or forgotten, I began tracing the outlines of Death Content- a distillation of the kinetic energy of those early live shows, and a simultaneous widening of the scope and sonic palette from the project’s earliest subterranean incarnation.

At the time that Vildava cohered and became more legible to me, I felt as if I was watching the world fall apart in real time. Live broadcasted genocide, the enclosure of our cities by private-equity parasites, technological warfare waged on our ecosystem and even our minds- no longer an abstraction, here with us now, in our homes and on the oily screens of our ubiquitous surveillance-entertainment devices. Earlier lyrical whimsy and lightheartedness gave way to stark, sometimes bleak reckonings with the hostage-situation of our economy, our implication in mass atrocity, and the dulling of our basic mental capacities.

Death Content is a statement of liberatory outrage; a furious rejection of a capitalist order that trades in the foreclosure of our collective future. This isn’t a hopeless vision, however. I believe in our community, in the vast and vibrant underground of artists that I am honored to call my peers. These living connections are grounded outside of the enclosed social-media landscape, fostered in basements, dive bars, in the cracks between our hours sold for rent. This is music for a future forged in common.”

Death Content was engineered and produced by Jaydee DeLeon at Enanoured Sound in Brooklyn, NY. Slabs of saturation coat monolithic sub-basslines recalling the work of The Bug and Justin Broadrick; leads ring out in endless tape decayed haze. Lawrence’s vocals jump from an earnest, sugary-melodic quiver to a howling death-growl in an instant, drawing from Noah Lennox, Ian Svenonius and Tom Warrior in equal measure. For all its wide-ranging influence in the global experimental and post-punk canon, Death Content is an undeniably New York record. Street and subway field recordings permeate the sonic landscape, clattering electronic drums and ferocious synth fuzz evoke a storied lineage of New York ear-abusers, from Suicide, Metal Machine Music and Bad Brains on through to early Uniform and the waveform destruction of Dreamcrusher. Like his peers in the New York underground, Vildava remains stubbornly uncategorizable; Death Content flips the switch from broken dembow grooves to smoldering black metal in an instant before settling into a dark techno-industrial dirge. In Death Content, Vildava seamlessly marries these disparate touchpoints with the singular intent of a New York City subterranean artist.
